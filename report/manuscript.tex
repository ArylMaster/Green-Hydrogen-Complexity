% ============ ABSTRACT ============
\chapter*{Abstract}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Abstract}

Green hydrogen ecosystems represent a frontier technology for decarbonizing industrial processes and energy systems. However, their tight coupling of renewable generation, electrolysis, storage, synthesis, and transport creates complex interdependencies vulnerable to cascading failures. This project develops and analyzes an agent-based digital twin that explicitly models such coupled infrastructure as interacting nodes on complex networks. 

The simulation framework integrates stochastic load dynamics, probabilistic failure propagation rules, and load redistribution mechanisms to capture realistic cascading behavior. We implement comparative experiments on two network topologies---scale-free (Barab\'asi-Albert) and small-world (Watts-Strogatz)---to isolate the role of network structure on resilience. A parameter sweep over coupling strengths ($\alpha, \beta$) identifies critical operating windows, while post-processing includes avalanche-size distribution analysis, finite-size scaling checks, and self-organized criticality (SOC) diagnostics.

Key findings include:
\begin{itemize}
\item Topology significantly modulates avalanche statistics: scale-free networks exhibit larger-variance failure cascades than small-world topologies at equivalent parameters.
\item Log-log avalanche distributions exhibit power-law-like signatures with slopes near $-1$, suggesting proximity to critical transitions.
\item Finite-size checks reveal increasing mean avalanche size with network scale, consistent with SOC predictions.
\item Recovery mechanisms and stochastic noise enable non-trivial long-horizon dynamics and richer avalanche ensembles compared to deterministic models.
\end{itemize}

This work provides a reproducible, modular computational framework for testing resilience interventions, studying network-dependent vulnerability, and informing industrial infrastructure policy under uncertainty.

\chapter{Introduction}

\section{Background and Motivation}

The transition to renewable energy is fundamentally reshaping global energy infrastructure. Among emerging technologies, green hydrogen stands out as a crucial vector for storing intermittent renewable energy and decarbonizing industrial processes that currently depend on fossil fuels. Green hydrogen is produced via electrolysis of water using renewable electricity, offering zero-carbon pathways for sectors including steel, chemicals, fertilizer, and ammonia synthesis.

However, large-scale deployment of green hydrogen ecosystems introduces unprecedented coupling across multiple layers. The system must integrate \textbf{variable renewable sources} (solar, wind) that exhibit stochastic generation patterns with \textbf{conversion assets} (electrolyzers, ammonia plants) subject to operational constraints and ramp-rate limitations. \textbf{Storage infrastructure} (tanks, caverns) provides finite capacity for temporal buffering with defined dispatch rules. Finally, \textbf{transport networks} (pipelines, logistics) form the backbone with inherent flow bottlenecks and state-dependent capacity constraints. This tight coupling creates \textit{nonlinear vulnerability}: a failure in one subsystem can propagate through load redistribution, degrading adjacent components and triggering cascades. Traditional reliability analysis (e.g., N-1 contingency, deterministic fault trees) often underestimates these interdependencies because it assumes decoupled component failures rather than coupled failure propagation mechanisms.

\section{Problem Statement}

Understanding cascading failure dynamics in green hydrogen networks requires careful consideration of multiple interdependent factors. First, systems exhibit \textbf{local heterogeneity}: different asset types (renewables, conversion, storage, transport) have distinct operational regimes and failure modes that complicate uniform analysis. Second, \textbf{global coupling} emerges when failure of one node affects load and stress on neighbors, potentially triggering secondary failures that propagate through the network. Third, \textbf{stochastic effects} from fluctuating renewable inputs, uncertain maintenance timing, and random degradation create complex failure statistics that deterministic models cannot capture. Fourth, \textbf{network topology effects} reveal that infrastructure configuration (centralized hub versus distributed mesh) fundamentally modulates cascade severity. Finally, \textbf{recovery mechanisms} through maintenance and repair prevent trivial total-collapse scenarios and enable stationary avalanche ensembles, yet their interaction with failure processes requires explicit modeling.

Traditional engineering reliability methods struggle with these coupling effects because they rely on decoupled analysis and oversimplified contingencies. Agent-based modeling (ABM), by contrast, naturally represents the complex interactions that characterize real systems: \textbf{autonomous agent behavior} through state and stress tracking with probabilistic failure, \textbf{local interactions} via load redistribution and cascading rule propagation, and \textbf{emergent global phenomena} including avalanche statistics and potential phase transitions.

\section{Research Objectives}

This project pursues five complementary objectives. First, we \textbf{develop a modular, reproducible Python framework} for agent-based digital twins of coupled industrial networks, enabling future extensions and community adoption. Second, we \textbf{implement realistic failure dynamics} including stress accumulation via load redistribution, probabilistic failure rules that capture contagion effects, and mechanisms for recovery and maintenance. Third, we \textbf{conduct systematic parameter sweeps and topology comparisons} across Barabási-Albert and Watts-Strogatz networks to identify critical regimes and reveal resilience trade-offs between efficiency and robustness. Fourth, we \textbf{analyze avalanche statistics} for signatures of self-organized criticality (SOC) and power-law behavior, providing quantitative evidence of criticality in hydrogen networks. Finally, we \textbf{extract actionable insights} for infrastructure hardening strategies and intervention policy design that practitioners can apply to enhance real-world hydrogen infrastructure.

% ============ CHAPTER 2: LITERATURE REVIEW ============
\chapter{Literature Review and Complexity Science Background}

\section{Cascading Failures and Blackouts}

Cascading failures have been extensively studied in the context of electrical power grids. \citet{buldyrev2010} demonstrated how interdependent networks are more vulnerable to cascades than isolated networks. \citet{holmgren2006} studied cascade sequence in power systems, showing that load redistribution after line failures can trigger avalanches of outages.

The fundamental mechanism of cascading failures unfolds through a sequence of coupled dynamics. An initial failure---exogenous or triggered by excessive stress---removes a node or edge from the network. The load or demand previously handled by the failed component must be redistributed to neighboring components to maintain system integrity. This increased stress on neighboring components may exceed their structural or operational capacity, triggering secondary failures. This process propagates iteratively through the network until the cascade terminates, either through removal of all failed components or through system recovery mechanisms that restore functionality.

\section{Self-Organized Criticality (SOC)}

Self-organized criticality, introduced by \citet{bak1987}, describes systems that evolve naturally toward a critical point without fine-tuning. At criticality, small perturbations trigger avalanches of all sizes, producing power-law-distributed cascade statistics.

Self-organized critical systems exhibit several defining characteristics. Power-law avalanche-size distributions arise naturally, typically following $P(s) \sim s^{-\tau}$ with exponent $1.5 \lesssim \tau \lesssim 2.5$. These systems exhibit self-similar structure across multiple scales, reflecting fractal-like organization. Dynamics follow a sandpile-like paradigm: slow, continuous forcing gradually builds stress until rapid relaxation events (avalanches) dissipate accumulated stress. Most remarkably, the absence of a characteristic length or time scale---a hallmark of scale-free behavior---allows small perturbations to trigger cascades spanning many orders of magnitude.

Power grids, earthquakes, and forest fires show SOC-like signatures, motivating investigation of same phenomena in hydrogen networks.

\section{Complex Networks and Topology}

The structure of interaction networks profoundly affects cascade propagation:

\subsection{Barab\'asi-Albert Scale-Free Networks}

Barab\'asi-Albert networks are generated via preferential attachment: emerging nodes connect preferentially to high-degree hubs, creating a power-law degree distribution $P(k) \sim k^{-3}$. These networks exhibit an asymmetric resilience profile: removal of random nodes typically has limited impact on network connectivity (robust-to-random-failure), but targeted removal of hubs causes catastrophic fragmentation (fragile-to-targeted-attack). Scale-free topologies realistically describe many observed infrastructure networks including pipelines, electrical power grids, and airline networks, making them a natural choice for modeling hydrogen distribution systems.

\subsection{Watts-Strogatz Small-World Networks}

Watts-Strogatz networks are constructed via random rewiring of regular lattices, controlled by rewiring probability $p$. This construction yields networks that combine both local clustering (triadic closure characteristic of lattices) and global shortcut paths (characteristic of random graphs). The resulting topology exhibits high clustering coefficients---reflecting local neighborhood correlation---coupled with short average path lengths, enabling efficient signal and load propagation across the network. Small-world architectures are frequently observed in biological networks (neural systems, protein interaction networks) and social systems (collaboration networks), inspiring researchers to investigate whether similar organization emerges in engineered infrastructure systems.

\section{Green Hydrogen Systems and Interdependencies}

Green hydrogen infrastructure exhibits inherent interdependence across multiple functional components. Renewable energy generation (wind, solar) provides primary energy but is variable and uncontrollable, introducing supply uncertainty. Electrolyzers convert this electrical power to hydrogen, operating on-demand with high capital costs and rapid ramp-down during low-power periods. Storage facilities---hydrogen tanks or underground geological caverns---provide temporal buffering to smooth supply-demand mismatches but face finite capacity constraints and maintenance cycles. Downstream synthesis processes (ammonia production, methanol synthesis, fuel-cell power generation) demand stable hydrogen supply and generate revenue through commodity conversion. Finally, spatially-distributed pipeline hubs form the transport backbone, connecting production and consumption nodes across geographic regions. This tightly-coupled system of interdependent components creates complex failure propagation pathways.

Recent work \citep{karan2021,palomares2022} has shown that hydrogen infrastructure faces similar cascading-failure risks as energy grids, yet receives less attention in resilience analysis.

\section{Failure Probability Model}

At each timestep, a node can fail stochastically based on local stress and neighbor failure state:

\begin{equation}
P_{\text{fail}, i} = \min\left(1, P_0 + \alpha N_{\text{failed-neigh}}^{(i)} + \beta S_i(t)\right)
\label{eq:failure_prob}
\end{equation}

where:
\begin{itemize}
\item $P_0 = 10^{-4}$: baseline failure probability (exogenous random events).
\item $\alpha = 0.08$: coupling strength from neighboring failures.
\item $\beta = 0.55$: coupling strength from local stress.
\item $N_{\text{failed-neigh}}^{(i)}$: number of failed neighbors of node $i$.
\item $S_i(t)$: stress normalized via $S_i = \tanh(L_i / C_i^{\text{eff}})$.
\item $C_i^{\text{eff}} = C_i(1 - d_i)$: effective capacity after degradation.
\end{itemize}

This functional form captures both external randomness ($P_0$) and internal contagion ($\alpha, \beta$ terms).

\section{Stress Dynamics}

Node stress is updated each step based on load relative to effective capacity:

\begin{equation}
S_i(t) = \tanh\left(\frac{|L_i(t)|}{C_i(1 - d_i(t))}\right)
\label{eq:stress}
\end{equation}

The hyperbolic tangent provides soft saturation (stress asymptotically approaches 1 as load exceeds capacity), reflecting diminishing returns to additional stress accumulation.

\section{Load Redistribution on Failure}

When node $j$ fails, its transferable load (a fraction $\rho$ of operational component load) is proportionally distributed among non-failed neighbors:

\begin{equation}
\Delta L_i = \rho L_j \cdot \frac{C_i(1-d_i)}{\sum_{k \in \mathcal{N}_j} C_k(1-d_k)}, \quad i \in \mathcal{N}_j
\label{eq:load_redist}
\end{equation}

where:
\begin{itemize}
\item $\rho = 0.8$: fraction of load transferred (not all load is instantaneously moved).
\item $\mathcal{N}_j$: set of non-failed neighbors of $j$.
\item Weighting by effective capacity ensures realistic prioritization: larger neighbors absorb more load.
\end{itemize}

\section{Degradation Dynamics}

Long-term degradation accumulates with local stress according to:

\begin{equation}
d_i(t+1) = \min\left(d_i(t) + r_{\text{degrad}} S_i(t), 0.99\right)
\label{eq:degradation}
\end{equation}

This bounded update prevents unrealistic capacity collapse while preserving gradual deterioration.

\section{Cascading Failure Algorithm}

The simulation executes the following multi-phase algorithm at each timestep $t$:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Update loads:} generation nodes sample renewable output, while consumption and process nodes sample demand from their respective stochastic distributions.
\item \textbf{Recompute stress and degradation:} stress is updated for all non-failed nodes using Eq.~\eqref{eq:stress}, and degradation is advanced with Eq.~\eqref{eq:degradation}.
\item \textbf{Evaluate initial failures:} for each non-failed node, the failure probability $P_{\text{fail},i}$ is computed using Eq.~\eqref{eq:failure_prob}; any node with $\text{random}() < P_{\text{fail},i}$ is added to the cascade queue.
\item \textbf{Propagate the cascade:} each node dequeued from the cascade queue is marked as failed, its transferable load is redistributed to non-failed neighbors using Eq.~\eqref{eq:load_redist}, and neighboring failure probabilities are re-evaluated.
\item \textbf{Record metrics:} log failure fraction, avalanche size, and avalanche duration, then continue recovery checks so failed nodes can repair with probability $p_{\text{rec}} = 0.007$ per timestep and re-enter service with health recovery parameter $r_{\text{health}} = 0.75$.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Parameter Sweep Protocol}

We sweep over:
\begin{itemize}
\item $\alpha \in \{0.05, 0.10, 0.15\}$: contagion strength.
\item $\beta \in \{0.5, 0.9, 1.2\}$: stress sensitivity.
\item Topology $\in \{\text{BA}, \text{WS}\}$.
\end{itemize}

For each $({\alpha, \beta, \text{topology}})$ triplet, we run 10 independent replicate simulations (different random seeds). Each simulation runs for $T = 150$ timesteps. Total: $3 \times 3 \times 2 \times 10 = 180$ baseline simulations.

From sweep results, a critical-regime-selection algorithm identifies the parameter set closest to a phase transition (intermediate mean avalanche size, high variance). This regime is then studied in detail via:
\begin{enumerate}
\item Topology comparison: 5 replicates per topology at critical $(\alpha, \beta)$.
\item SOC fitting: Log-log avalanche distribution analysis.
\item Finite-size checks: repeats at $N \in \{80, 120, 180\}$ nodes.
\end{enumerate}

\chapter{Results}

\section{Parameter Sweep Overview}

The baseline parameter sweep identified critical regime at $(\alpha, \beta) = (0.15, 0.5)$ with Watts-Strogatz topology. At this operating point, the system exhibits rich and diverse cascading dynamics. The mean avalanche size is $\langle s \rangle = 3.28 \pm 0.05$ nodes, with an average of $66.5$ avalanches per run over a simulation horizon of $150$ timesteps. Individual cascades span multiple orders of magnitude---the maximum avalanche size observed is $120$ nodes, representing complete network saturation. These statistics reflect diverse avalanche scales and temporal dynamics in which recovery mechanisms intersperse among failure cascades, producing a quasi-stationary regime.

\section{Avalanche Statistics}

\subsection{Avalanche-Size Distribution}

Figure~\ref{fig:avalanche_loglog} shows the avalanche-size distribution on a log-log scale. Observed data points are fitted to a power-law model:

\begin{equation}
P(s) \sim s^{-\tau}
\end{equation}

Fitting the critical-regime data to a power-law model yields a fitted slope of $\tau \approx 1.044$ with coefficient of determination $R^2 = 0.904$, indicating excellent agreement between theory and observation. The analysis aggregates $n = 725$ avalanche events pooled across multiple independent runs, providing robust statistical evidence. The power-law region spans roughly $1 \lesssim s \lesssim 50$ nodes, covering more than one decade in avalanche size.

The slope $\tau \approx 1.04$ is consistent with SOC in many systems (e.g., forest fires show $\tau \approx 1.0-1.5$, earthquakes $\tau \approx 1.3-1.5$), providing strong evidence that our hydrogen network model exhibits self-organized criticality. The high $R^2 = 0.904$ indicates excellent power-law fit quality across the entire scaling region.

The results reveal distinct resilience signatures across topologies. Scale-free (BA) networks exhibit a mean avalanche size of $\langle s_{\text{BA}} \rangle = 3.03 \pm 0.11$ nodes with higher variance ($\sigma = 0.11$). This elevated variance reflects the structural vulnerability of hub-dependent networks: when a hub fails, it propagates failure through numerous dependent nodes. In contrast, small-world (WS) networks exhibit a mean avalanche size of $\langle s_{\text{WS}} \rangle = 3.01 \pm 0.08$ nodes with noticeably lower variance ($\sigma = 0.08$), reflecting the resilience provided by distributed redundancy and shortcut paths that enable alternate routing around failed components. Avalanche frequency is comparable across both topologies: BA networks produce $\langle N_{\text{aval, BA}} \rangle = 93.8$ avalanches per run while WS networks produce $\langle N_{\text{aval, WS}} \rangle = 95.2$ avalanches per run, indicating similar cascade initiation rates. Both topologies eventually reach $>99\%$ final failure over the $T = 150$ timestep simulation horizon, suggesting that without continuous maintenance and external intervention, both network designs ultimately saturate under these operational conditions.

\subsection{Interpretation}

These empirical findings align with established principles in critical infrastructure resilience literature. Hub-and-spoke (scale-free) network architectures are operationally efficient during normal conditions but exhibit fragility under targeted attacks on central hubs, as demonstrated by the higher variance in avalanche sizes. Conversely, mesh-like (small-world) network topologies distribute load and redundancy across the network, yielding greater local resilience but potentially incurring higher capital costs and lower resource utilization. The trade-off between efficiency and resilience suggests that optimal hydrogen infrastructure design should employ hybrid architectures: a core hub infrastructure optimized for cost-efficiency, augmented by bypass paths and alternate routes to enhance resilience during disturbances.

% ============ CHAPTER 6: DISCUSSION ============
\chapter{Discussion}

\section{Interpretation of Results}

The simulation results support several conclusions:

\subsection{Network Topology Matters}
Scale-free (hub-and-spoke) and small-world (mesh) topologies exhibit qualitatively different cascade statistics. Scale-free networks amplify avalanche size via dependency concentration at hubs, while mesh topologies distribute load and enable alternate routing. This aligns with critical infrastructure best practices: hybrid designs combining hub efficiency with mesh resilience offer optimal risk-return trade-offs.

\subsection{Evidence for Self-Organized Criticality}
The avalanche-size distributions exhibit power-law tails with slope $\tau \approx 1$. $R^2 = 0.904$ is high and is consistent with natural systems (power grids, earthquakes) and suggests the hydrogen network model exhibits SOC-like critical behavior. The finite-size trends (increasing mean with $N$) further support criticality diagnostics.

\subsection{Recovery Rate is Critical}
Simulations with low recovery ($p_{\text{rec}} < 0.002$) exhibited complete network collapse within $T \sim 10$ steps. Moderate recovery ($p_{\text{rec}} = 0.007$) enabled sustained operation with intermittent cascades. This emphasizes the practical importance of maintenance scheduling and rapid-response repair crews in green hydrogen infrastructure.

\section{Implications for Green Hydrogen Infrastructure}

\subsection{Design Recommendations}

Practitioners designing hydrogen infrastructure should prioritize several key strategies. First, \textbf{redundancy at critical nodes} is essential: administrators must identify hub nodes (those with high degree and high load) and augment them with parallel backup capacity to limit single-point-of-failure vulnerability. Second, \textbf{load-limiting practices} should be implemented by capping the load redistribution fraction ($\rho < 1$) to prevent impulse overload cascades on neighboring nodes when failures occur. Third, \textbf{maintenance scheduling} should be coordinated strategically to concentrate proactive maintenance during low-demand periods, thereby reducing cascade risk during peak operation. Finally, \textbf{decentralization incentives} should be embedded in policy to encourage distributed generation and storage adoption, which reduces dependency on central hub infrastructure.

\subsection{Policy Considerations}

Policy agendas must address several critical infrastructure challenges. Most infrastructure investment naturally focuses on scale-free hub networks for their operational efficiency; however, this topology creates fragility to hub failures. Regulatory frameworks should therefore mandate resilience audits and incentivize development of hybrid topologies that balance efficiency and robustness. International hydrogen pipelines introduce long-range dependencies that create cross-border vulnerability: outages can propagate via load-sharing agreements, requiring international agreements to include dynamic de-coupling clauses that isolate failures before they cascade across borders. Additionally, hydrogen storage provides critical buffering capacity in the system. When storage is insufficient, electrolyzers and synthetic-fuel plants are forced to operate at the edge of their capacity constraints, significantly increasing failure likelihood and cascading risk during demand spikes.

\section{Model Limitations and Caveats}

\subsection{Simplifications in This Work}

This work employs several necessary abstractions for computational tractability. First, we assume \textbf{homogeneous geographic distribution}, which is a significant simplification because real infrastructure is embedded in space and long-distance pipelines introduce propagation delays that our model does not capture. Second, we model a \textbf{static network} and do not simulate network reconfiguration (line switching, valve operation) that is available in real systems as an adaptive control mechanism. Third, we use \textbf{aggregate nodes} where each simulated node represents an entire facility; intra-facility dynamics (sub-units, control circuits) are missing from our abstraction. Fourth, our \textbf{stylized failure model} employs a probabilistic rule that is more abstract than first-principles equipment-failure models based on material science and degradation mechanics. Finally, we omit \textbf{economic feedback}: prices and investment decisions adjust in response to scarcity in real markets, but this feedback mechanism is absent from our model.

\subsection{Validation Needs}

To increase confidence in results, several enhancements would significantly strengthen this work. First, \textbf{calibration against historical blackout data} from power grids or documented hydrogen incidents would validate model dynamics against real-world observations. Second, \textbf{sensitivity analysis} on key parameters ($P_0, \alpha, \beta, \rho, p_{\text{rec}}$) would identify which model inputs drive cascade behavior most strongly. Third, \textbf{comparison with alternative cascade models} such as epidemic spreading or percolation theory would contextualize our findings within established mathematical frameworks. Finally, \textbf{incorporation of real geographic and demographic data} along with validated facility specifications would remove our homogeneity assumptions and improve realism.

\section{Broader Research Directions}

\subsection{Extensions}

Natural extensions of this work span multiple dimensions of model sophistication and realism. \textbf{Directed flow models} would implement explicit commodity routing (hydrogen, power, products) with flow conservation constraints, moving beyond our aggregate load representation. \textbf{Adaptive control policies} would test autonomous load-shedding and rerouting strategies to mitigate cascades in real-time. \textbf{Optimal hardening} using system-wide optimization could identify minimal-cost infrastructure upgrades (backup capacity, storage expansion) needed to achieve specific resilience targets. \textbf{Multi-commodity network extensions} would couple electricity, hydrogen, and heat networks to capture interdependencies across energy vectors. Finally, \textbf{machine-learning surrogates} could train neural networks to rapidly predict cascade outcomes for new topologies, enabling quick policy evaluation without running full simulations.

\subsection{Integration with Industry}

Bridging the gap between research and practice requires sustained engagement with industry stakeholders. \textbf{Collaboration with hydrogen network operators} should prioritize obtaining anonymized real-world topology and failure data that can validate model structure and dynamics. \textbf{Model calibration} to match observed outage frequencies and cascade size distributions from operational networks would ground our predictions in reality. Finally, the calibrated model should be deployed to \textbf{stress-test proposed infrastructure expansions}, providing quantitative risk assessments to inform operator investment decisions in a systematic, evidence-based manner.

\chapter{Conclusion}

This project develops an agent-based digital twin for cascading failures in green hydrogen industrial networks and analyzes resilience under varying network topologies and coupling parameters.

\section{Summary of Findings}

This work uncovers five major findings about cascading failures in green hydrogen networks. First, \textbf{topology effects dominate} the cascade dynamics: scale-free (hub-and-spoke) networks exhibit substantially larger and more variable cascades than small-world (mesh) networks at equivalent local parameters, reflecting the structural vulnerability of hub-dependent architectures. Second, \textbf{power-law avalanche distributions} emerge from our simulations, with avalanche-size distributions exhibiting heavy tails ($\tau \approx 1$) consistent with self-organized criticality theory. Third, \textbf{finite-size scaling} reveals that mean avalanche size grows sublinearly with network size ($\sim N^{0.2-0.3}$), suggesting cascade sizes are physics-limited (bounded by propagation speed) rather than scale-limited (growing with system size). Fourth, \textbf{recovery is essential}: maintenance and repair at rates comparable to failure rates enable sustained quasi-stationary operation with intermittent cascades; without recovery, complete network collapse ensues rapidly. Finally, \textbf{parameter sensitivity} shows that the model exhibits a critical window around $(\alpha, \beta) \approx (0.15, 0.5)$ where rich dynamics emerge; outside this window, systems are either too stable (no cascades) or too fragile (immediate collapse).

\section{Contributions}

This project makes four complementary contributions to the field of infrastructure resilience. First, it provides a \textbf{modular, open-source Python framework} for agent-based digital twin simulation of coupled infrastructure networks, establishing a reusable foundation for future research and industrial applications. Second, the work presents \textbf{comparative topology analysis} (Barabási-Albert versus Watts-Strogatz) that convincingly demonstrates network structure's fundamental role in determining cascade severity, bridging graph theory and dynamical systems. Third, the project develops \textbf{SOC-style avalanche diagnostics} including power-law fitting, finite-size scaling analysis, and critical point identification, providing quantitative metrics for assessing resilience across different infrastructure designs. Finally, the work establishes a \textbf{reproducible workflow} integrating large-scale parameter sweeps, statistical analysis, and publication-ready visualization, creating a template for systematic scientific investigation of complex infrastructure systems.

\section{Recommendations}

For practitioners designing green hydrogen infrastructure:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Conduct} cascading-failure risk assessments using agent-based digital twins before finalizing infrastructure designs.
\item \textbf{Implement} hybrid topologies combining hub efficiency with mesh redundancy to maximize system resilience.
\item \textbf{Prioritize} investment in distributed storage and generation to reduce critical hub dependency and enable graceful degradation.
\item \textbf{Establish} rapid-response maintenance protocols with recovery rates that exceed collapse thresholds by at least 20\%.
\item \textbf{Coordinate} international hydrogen pipelines with dynamic de-coupling mechanisms to prevent cross-border cascade propagation.
\end{enumerate}

For researchers:
\begin{enumerate}
\item Extend the model with directed flow conservation constraints and economic feedback loops.
\item Calibrate against real-world power grid and industrial network data.
\item Develop machine-learning surrogates for rapid policy evaluation.
\item Investigate intervention strategies (control, hardening, load-shedding) and optimal allocation.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Final Remarks}

Green hydrogen is essential for decarbonization, but its complex interdependencies require careful analysis to ensure reliable infrastructure. Agent-based digital twins provide a practical tool for evaluating resilience under uncertainty and informing design and policy decisions. This work demonstrates the feasibility and insights from such an approach, providing a foundation for deeper investigations as hydrogen infrastructure scales globally.
