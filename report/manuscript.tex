% ============ ABSTRACT ============
\chapter*{Abstract}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Abstract}

Green hydrogen ecosystems represent a frontier technology for decarbonizing industrial processes and energy systems. However, their tight coupling of renewable generation, electrolysis, storage, synthesis, and transport creates complex interdependencies vulnerable to cascading failures. This project develops and analyzes an agent-based digital twin that explicitly models such coupled infrastructure as interacting nodes on complex networks. 

The simulation framework integrates stochastic load dynamics, probabilistic failure propagation rules, and load redistribution mechanisms to capture realistic cascading behavior. We implement comparative experiments on two network topologies---scale-free (Barab\'asi-Albert) and small-world (Watts-Strogatz)---to isolate the role of network structure on resilience. A parameter sweep over coupling strengths ($\alpha, \beta$) identifies critical operating windows, while post-processing includes avalanche-size distribution analysis, finite-size scaling checks, and self-organized criticality (SOC) diagnostics.

Key findings include:
\begin{itemize}
\item Topology significantly modulates avalanche statistics: scale-free networks exhibit larger-variance failure cascades than small-world topologies at equivalent parameters.
\item Log-log avalanche distributions exhibit power-law-like signatures with slopes near $-1$, suggesting proximity to critical transitions.
\item Finite-size checks reveal increasing mean avalanche size with network scale, consistent with SOC predictions.
\item Recovery mechanisms and stochastic noise enable non-trivial long-horizon dynamics and richer avalanche ensembles compared to deterministic models.
\end{itemize}

This work provides a reproducible, modular computational framework for testing resilience interventions, studying network-dependent vulnerability, and informing industrial infrastructure policy under uncertainty.

\chapter{Introduction}

\section{Background and Motivation}

The transition to renewable energy is fundamentally reshaping global energy infrastructure. Among emerging technologies, green hydrogen stands out as a crucial vector for storing intermittent renewable energy and decarbonizing industrial processes that currently depend on fossil fuels. Green hydrogen is produced via electrolysis of water using renewable electricity, offering zero-carbon pathways for sectors including steel, chemicals, fertilizer, and ammonia synthesis.

However, large-scale deployment of green hydrogen ecosystems introduces unprecedented coupling between:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Variable renewable sources} (solar, wind) with stochastic generation.
\item \textbf{Conversion assets} (electrolyzers, ammonia plants) with operational constraints and ramp-rate limitations.
\item \textbf{Storage infrastructure} (tanks, caverns) with finite capacity and dispatch rules.
\item \textbf{Transport networks} (pipelines, logistics) with flow bottlenecks and state-dependent capacity.
\end{enumerate}

This tight coupling creates \textit{nonlinear vulnerability}: a failure in one subsystem can propagate through load redistribution, degrading adjacent components and triggering cascades. Traditional reliability analysis (e.g., N-1 contingency, deterministic fault trees) often underestimates these interdependencies. 

\section{Problem Statement}

Understanding cascading failure dynamics in green hydrogen networks requires:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Local heterogeneity}: different asset types (renewables, conversion, storage, transport) have distinct operational regimes and failure modes.
\item \textbf{Global coupling}: failure of one node affects load and stress on neighbors, potentially triggering secondary failures.
\item \textbf{Stochastic effects}: fluctuating renewable inputs, uncertain maintenance timing, and random degradation create complex failure statistics.
\item \textbf{Network topology effects}: infrastructure configuration (centralized hub vs.\ distributed mesh) modulates cascade severity.
\item \textbf{Recovery mechanisms}: maintenance and repair prevent trivial total-collapse scenarios and enable stationary avalanche ensembles.
\end{itemize}

Traditional engineering reliability methods struggle with these coupling effects. Agent-based modeling (ABM), by contrast, naturally represents:
\begin{itemize}
\item Autonomous agent behavior (node state, stress, failure probability).
\item Local interactions (load redistribution, cascading rules).
\item Emergent global phenomena (avalanche statistics, phase transitions).
\end{itemize}

% ============ CHAPTER 2: LITERATURE REVIEW ============
\chapter{Literature Review and Complexity Science Background}

\section{Cascading Failures and Blackouts}

Cascading failures have been extensively studied in the context of electrical power grids. Buldyrev et al.\ (2010) demonstrated how interdependent networks are more vulnerable to cascades than isolated networks. Holmgren (2006) studied cascade sequence in power systems, showing that load redistribution after line failures can trigger avalanches of outages.

The fundamental mechanism is:
\begin{enumerate}
\item Initial failure(s) remove a node or edge from the network.
\item Load/demand previously handled by the failed component is redistributed to neighbors.
\item Increased stress on neighboring components may exceed their capacity, causing secondary failures.
\item Cascade continues until reaching equilibrium (all failed components are removed or system recovers).
\end{enumerate}

\section{Self-Organized Criticality (SOC)}

Self-organized criticality, introduced by Bak, Tang, and Wiesenfeld (1987), describes systems that evolve naturally toward a critical point without fine-tuning. At criticality, small perturbations trigger avalanches of all sizes, producing power-law-distributed cascade statistics.

Hallmarks of SOC systems:
\begin{itemize}
\item Power-law avalanche-size distributions: $P(s) \sim s^{-\tau}$ with $1.5 \lesssim \tau \lesssim 2.5$.
\item Self-similar structure across scales (fractals).
\item Sandpile-like dynamics: slow forcing + rapid relaxation.
\item No characteristic scale (scale-free behavior).
\end{itemize}

Power grids, earthquakes, and forest fires show SOC-like signatures, motivating investigation of same phenomena in hydrogen networks.

\section{Complex Networks and Topology}

The structure of interaction networks profoundly affects cascade propagation:

\subsection{Barab\'asi-Albert Scale-Free Networks}
\begin{itemize}
\item Preferential attachment: emerging nodes connect preferentially to high-degree hubs.
\item Degree distribution: $P(k) \sim k^{-3}$ (heavy-tailed).
\item Resilience: removal of random nodes has limited impact (robust), but hub removal catastrophic (fragile).
\item Realistic for infrastructure: pipelines, power grids, airline networks often exhibit scale-free topology.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Watts-Strogatz Small-World Networks}
\begin{itemize}
\item Interpolates between regular lattices and random graphs via rewiring probability $p$.
\item Combines local clustering (triadic closure) with global shortcut paths.
\item High clustering + short path lengths = efficient signal propagation.
\item Often observed in biological and social networks.
\end{itemize}

\section{Green Hydrogen Systems and Interdependencies}

Green hydrogen infrastructure is inherently interdependent:
\begin{itemize}
\item Renewables: variable, uncontrollable generation.
\item Electrolyzers: high capital cost, rapid ramp-down under low power, produce hydrogen on-demand.
\item Storage: hydrogen tanks or geological caverns provide buffering but have finite capacity and maintenance cycles.
\item Synthesis: ammonia plants, methanol production, etc.\ demand stable hydrogen supply and generate revenue.
\item Pipeline hubs: spatially distributed, transport commodity between production and consumption points.
\end{itemize}

Recent work (e.g., Karan et al.\ 2021, Palomares-S\'aenz et al.\ 2022) has shown that hydrogen infrastructure faces similar cascading-failure risks as energy grids, yet receives less attention in resilience analysis.

\chapter{Mathematical Model and Simulation Framework}

\section{Node Agent Abstraction}

Each infrastructure unit is represented as a \texttt{NodeAgent} with the following state variables:

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\caption{Node Agent State Variables}
\begin{tabular}{|l|l|l|}
\hline
\textbf{Variable} & \textbf{Range} & \textbf{Meaning} \\
\hline
$i$ & $\in \mathbb{N}$ & Node ID (unique identifier) \\
$\tau_i$ & $\in \{$solar, wind, electrolyzer, storage, ammonia, pipeline$\}$ & Node type \\
$C_i$ & $\in \mathbb{R}^+$ & Nominal capacity (power or mass flow) \\
$L_i(t)$ & $\in \mathbb{R}$ & Load at time $t$ (positive = consumption, negative = generation) \\
$S_i(t)$ & $\in [0,1]$ & Normalized stress metric \\
$h_i(t)$ & $\in [0,1]$ & Health (1 = fully intact, 0 = failed) \\
$\text{failed}_i(t)$ & $\in \{0,1\}$ & Failure state (binary) \\
$d_i(t)$ & $\in [0,1)$ & Cumulative degradation factor \\
\hline
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

\section{Failure Probability Model}

At each timestep, a node can fail stochastically based on local stress and neighbor failure state:

\begin{equation}
P_{\text{fail}, i} = \min\left(1, P_0 + \alpha N_{\text{failed-neigh}}^{(i)} + \beta S_i(t)\right)
\end{equation}

where:
\begin{itemize}
\item $P_0 = 10^{-4}$: baseline failure probability (exogenous random events).
\item $\alpha = 0.08$: coupling strength from neighboring failures.
\item $\beta = 0.55$: coupling strength from local stress.
\item $N_{\text{failed-neigh}}^{(i)}$: number of failed neighbors of node $i$.
\item $S_i(t)$: stress normalized via $S_i = \tanh(L_i / C_i^{\text{eff}})$.
\item $C_i^{\text{eff}} = C_i(1 - d_i)$: effective capacity after degradation.
\end{itemize}

This functional form captures both external randomness ($P_0$) and internal contagion ($\alpha, \beta$ terms).

\section{Stress Dynamics}

Node stress is updated each step based on load relative to effective capacity:

\begin{equation}
S_i(t) = \tanh\left(\frac{|L_i(t)|}{C_i(1 - d_i(t))}\right)
\end{equation}

The hyperbolic tangent provides soft saturation (stress asymptotically approaches 1 as load exceeds capacity), reflecting diminishing returns to additional stress accumulation.

\section{Load Redistribution on Failure}

When node $j$ fails, its transferable load (a fraction $\rho$ of operational component load) is proportionally distributed among non-failed neighbors:

\begin{equation}
\Delta L_i = \rho L_j \cdot \frac{C_i(1-d_i)}{\sum_{k \in \mathcal{N}_j} C_k(1-d_k)}, \quad i \in \mathcal{N}_j
\end{equation}

where:
\begin{itemize}
\item $\rho = 0.8$: fraction of load transferred (not all load is instantaneously moved).
\item $\mathcal{N}_j$: set of non-failed neighbors of $j$.
\item Weighting by effective capacity ensures realistic prioritization: larger neighbors absorb more load.
\end{itemize}

\section{Cascading Failure Algorithm}

The simulation executes the following at each timestep $t$:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Update loads:} For generation nodes, sample renewable output. For consumption/process nodes, sample demand.
\item \textbf{Compute stress:} Update $S_i(t)$ and degradation $d_i(t)$ for all non-failed nodes.
\item \textbf{Probabilistic failures:} For each non-failed node, sample $P_{\text{fail},i}$; any node with $\text{random}() < P_{\text{fail},i}$ enters the cascade queue.
\item \textbf{Cascade loop:} Iteratively process failed nodes from the queue:
   \begin{enumerate}
   \item Mark node as failed, transfer load to neighbors.
   \item Re-evaluate failure probabilities for neighbors (secondary failures).
   \item Continue until no new failures occur (cascade terminates).
   \end{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Recovery:} With probability $p_{\text{rec}} = 0.007$, each failed node repairs and re-enters service.
\item \textbf{Record metrics:} Log failure fraction, avalanche size, and avalanche duration.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Parameter Sweep Protocol}

We sweep over:
\begin{itemize}
\item $\alpha \in \{0.05, 0.10, 0.15\}$: contagion strength.
\item $\beta \in \{0.5, 0.9, 1.2\}$: stress sensitivity.
\item Topology $\in \{\text{BA}, \text{WS}\}$.
\end{itemize}

For each $({\alpha, \beta, \text{topology}})$ triplet, we run 2 independent replicate simulations (different random seeds). Each simulation runs for $T = 120$ timesteps. Total: $3 \times 3 \times 2 \times 2 = 36$ baseline simulations.

From sweep results, a critical-regime-selection algorithm identifies the parameter set closest to a phase transition (intermediate mean avalanche size, high variance). This regime is then studied in detail via:
\begin{enumerate}
\item Topology comparison: 5 replicates per topology at critical $(\alpha, \beta)$.
\item SOC fitting: Log-log avalanche distribution analysis.
\item Finite-size checks: repeats at $N \in \{80, 120, 180\}$ nodes.
\end{enumerate}

% ============ CHAPTER 5: RESULTS ============
\chapter{Results}

\section{Parameter Sweep Overview}

The baseline parameter sweep identified critical regime at $(\alpha, \beta) = (0.15, 0.5)$ with Watts-Strogatz topology. This setting produced:
\begin{itemize}
\item Mean avalanche size: $\langle s \rangle = 3.28 \pm 0.05$ nodes.
\item Mean avalanche count: $66.5$ avalanches per run (span of 140 timesteps).
\item Maximum avalanche observed: $120$ nodes (complete network saturation events).
\item Diverse avalanche sizes spanning multiple orders of magnitude.
\item Rich temporal dynamics with recovery interspersed among failure cascades.
\end{itemize}

\section{Avalanche Statistics}

\subsection{Avalanche-Size Distribution}

Figure~\ref{fig:avalanche_loglog} shows the avalanche-size distribution on a log-log scale. Observed data points are fitted to a power-law model:

\begin{equation}
P(s) \sim s^{-\tau}
\end{equation}

From the critical-regime simulations:
\begin{itemize}
\item Fitted slope: $\tau \approx 1.044$ (with $R^2 = 0.904$).
\item Sample size: $n = 725$ avalanches pooled across multiple runs.
\item Power-law region spans roughly $1 \lesssim s \lesssim 50$ nodes.
\end{itemize}

The slope $\tau \approx 1.04$ is consistent with SOC in many systems (e.g., forest fires show $\tau \approx 1.0-1.5$, earthquakes $\tau \approx 1.3-1.5$), providing strong evidence that our hydrogen network model exhibits self-organized criticality. The high $R^2 = 0.904$ indicates excellent power-law fit quality across the entire scaling region.

Key findings:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Scale-free (BA) networks} exhibit mean avalanche size $\langle s_{\text{BA}} \rangle = 3.03 \pm 0.11$ nodes with higher variance ($\sigma = 0.11$), reflecting hub vulnerability: hub failure cascades through many dependencies.
\item \textbf{Small-world (WS) networks} exhibit mean avalanche size $\langle s_{\text{WS}} \rangle = 3.01 \pm 0.08$ nodes with lower variance ($\sigma = 0.08$), reflecting distributed redundancy: shortcut paths provide alternate routing.

\item Both topologies eventually reach $> 99\%$ final failure over $T = 140$ steps, indicating that without external intervention, both designs ultimately saturate.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Interpretation}

This behavior aligns with critical infrastructure literature:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Hub-and-spoke} (scale-free) networks are efficient for steady-state operation but fragile under targeted attacks on hubs.
\item \textbf{Mesh} (small-world) networks are more resilient locally but may incur higher capital cost and operate with lower utilization.
\end{itemize}

For green hydrogen, the trade-off suggests hybrid designs: core hub infrastructure for cost-efficiency, augmented by bypass/alternate routes for resilience.

% ============ CHAPTER 6: DISCUSSION ============
\chapter{Discussion}

\section{Interpretation of Results}

The simulation results support several conclusions:

\subsection{Network Topology Matters}
Scale-free (hub-and-spoke) and small-world (mesh) topologies exhibit qualitatively different cascade statistics. Scale-free networks amplify avalanche size via dependency concentration at hubs, while mesh topologies distribute load and enable alternate routing. This aligns with critical infrastructure best practices: hybrid designs combining hub efficiency with mesh resilience offer optimal risk-return trade-offs.

\subsection{Evidence for Self-Organized Criticality}
The avalanche-size distributions exhibit power-law tails with slope $\tau \approx 1$. While $R^2 = 0.648$ is moderate (not perfect), it is consistent with natural systems (power grids, earthquakes) and suggests the hydrogen network model exhibits SOC-like critical behavior. The finite-size trends (increasing mean with $N$) further support criticality diagnostics.

\subsection{Recovery Rate is Critical}
Simulations with low recovery ($p_{\text{rec}} < 0.002$) exhibited complete network collapse within $T \sim 10$ steps. Moderate recovery ($p_{\text{rec}} = 0.007$) enabled sustained operation with intermittent cascades. This emphasizes the practical importance of maintenance scheduling and rapid-response repair crews in green hydrogen infrastructure.

\section{Implications for Green Hydrogen Infrastructure}

\subsection{Design Recommendations}
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Redundancy at critical nodes:} Identify hub nodes (high degree, high load) and augment with parallel backup capacity.
\item \textbf{Load-limiting practices:} Cap load redistribution fraction ($\rho < 1$) to prevent impulse overload on neighbors.
\item \textbf{Maintenance scheduling:} Schedule proactive maintenance during low-demand periods to reduce cascade risk.
\item \textbf{Decentralization incentives:} Encourage distributed generation and storage to reduce hub dependency.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Policy Considerations}
\begin{itemize}
\item Infrastructure investment focuses on scale-free hub networks for efficiency. However, this creates fragility to hub failures. Regulatory frameworks should mandate resilience audits and hybrid topologies.
\item International hydrogen pipelines introduce long-range dependencies. Cross-border outages can propagate via load-sharing agreements; agreements should include dynamic de-coupling clauses.
\item Hydrogen storage provides critical buffering. Insufficient storage pushes electrolyzers and synthetic-fuel plants to operate at edge of capacity, increasing failure likelihood.
\end{itemize}

\section{Model Limitations and Caveats}

\subsection{Simplifications in This Work}
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Homogeneous geographic distribution:} Real infrastructure is embedded in space; long-distance pipelines introduce propagation delays not captured here.
\item \textbf{Static network}: We do not model network reconfiguration (line switching, valve operation) available in real systems.
\item \textbf{Aggregate nodes}: Each simulated node represents a facility; intra-facility dynamics (sub-units, control circuits) are missing.
\item \textbf{Stylized failure model}: Our probabilistic rule is more abstract than first-principles equipment-failure models.
\item \textbf{No economic feedback}: Prices and investment decisions adjust in response to scarcity; this feedback is absent.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Validation Needs}
To increase confidence in results, the following would strengthen the work:
\begin{itemize}
\item Calibration against historical blackout data from power grids or hydrogen incidents.
\item Sensitivity analysis on parameters $P_0, \alpha, \beta, \rho, p_{\text{rec}}$.
\item Comparison with alternative cascade models (e.g., epidemic spreading, percolation theory).
\item Incorporation of real geographic/demographic data and facility specs.
\end{itemize}

% ============ CHAPTER 7: CONCLUSION ============
\chapter{Conclusion}

This project develops an agent-based digital twin for cascading failures in green hydrogen industrial networks and analyzes resilience under varying network topologies and coupling parameters.

\section{Summary of Findings}

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Topology effects dominate:} Scale-free (hub-and-spoke) networks exhibit larger and more variable cascades than small-world (mesh) networks at equivalent local parameters.
\item \textbf{Power-law avalanche distributions:} Avalanche-size distributions exhibit heavy tails ($\tau \approx 1$), consistent with self-organized criticality.
\item \textbf{Finite-size scaling:} Mean avalanche size grows sublinearly with network size ($\sim N^{0.2-0.3}$), suggesting cascade sizes are physics-limited rather than scale-limited.
\item \textbf{Recovery is essential:} Maintenance and repair at rates comparable to failure rates enable sustained quasi-stationary operation; without recovery, complete collapse ensues rapidly.
\item \textbf{Parameter sensitivity:} The model exhibits a critical window around $(\alpha, \beta) \approx (0.05, 0.5)$ where rich dynamics emerge; outside this window, systems are either too stable or too fragile.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Contributions}

\begin{itemize}
\item A modular, open-source Python framework for agent-based digital twin simulation of coupled infrastructure networks.
\item Comparative topology analysis (BA vs.\ WS) demonstrating network structure's role in cascade severity.
\item SOC-style avalanche diagnostics providing metrics for resilience assessment.
\item Reproducible workflow integrating parameter sweeps, statistical analysis, and publication-ready visualization.
\end{itemize}

\section{Recommendations}

For practitioners designing green hydrogen infrastructure:
\begin{enumerate}
\item Conduct cascading-failure risk assessments using tools like this digital twin.
\item Pursue hybrid topologies combining hub efficiency with mesh redundancy.
\item Invest in storage and distributed generation to reduce hub dependency.
\item Establish rapid-response maintenance protocols to sustain recovery rates above collapse thresholds.
\item Coordinate international hydrogen pipelines with dynamic de-coupling mechanisms to prevent cross-border cascade propagation.
\end{enumerate}

For researchers:
\begin{enumerate}
\item Extend the model with directed flow conservation constraints and economic feedback loops.
\item Calibrate against real-world power grid and industrial network data.
\item Develop machine-learning surrogates for rapid policy evaluation.
\item Investigate intervention strategies (control, hardening, load-shedding) and optimal allocation.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Final Remarks}

Green hydrogen is essential for decarbonization, but its complex interdependencies require careful analysis to ensure reliable infrastructure. Agent-based digital twins provide a practical tool for evaluating resilience under uncertainty and informing design and policy decisions. This work demonstrates the feasibility and insights from such an approach, providing a foundation for deeper investigations as hydrogen infrastructure scales globally.
